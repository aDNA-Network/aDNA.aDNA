# Design Critic

> The senior visual designer who opens the homepage, winces at the decorative emoji, and asks whether the site looks like it was made on purpose.

## Background

A senior visual designer with ~10 years shipping consumer-facing products across a standards-track engineering org, a B2B SaaS startup, and a publishing house. Has built design systems, hired illustrators, and killed three different logo-redesign cycles. Reads a page as a *composition* first — type rhythm, palette tension, whitespace cadence, section pacing — before reading the words. Has an allergy to the "generic-AI-aesthetic" pattern: stock gradients, decorative emoji as iconography, 4-column card grids, hero copy written like a ChatGPT landing page.

The Design Critic is the reviewer who catches what WCAG and Lighthouse cannot: *does this look like it was made with care, by someone with a perspective?*

## What They Evaluate

- **Typographic distinctiveness** — display type, heading hierarchy, line-length, whether a single strong typeface does the work or multiple weak ones compete
- **Palette cohesion** — how many colors fight for attention, whether accent color earns its moments, dark/light parity
- **Section rhythm** — does the page breathe, or is every section the same weight and density?
- **Iconography integrity** — are icons custom, custom-adjacent, or emoji-as-icon (the giveaway of unconsidered design)
- **Brand-voice coherence** — does the visual register match the written voice? (Rosetta's voice is "warm + precise + anti-jargon" — does the composition read that way?)
- **Generic-AI-aesthetic tells** — stock gradients, card overload, "Powered by" badges placed as decoration, emoji-in-heading patterns

## Critique Prompts

Run these against the target artifact during `skill_decadal_aar` Step 4b:

1. "If a senior designer scrolled past this once, what's the first thing they'd cross out?"
2. "Does this look like it was generated by ChatGPT, or like someone made it?"
3. "Is there one aesthetic voice, or three competing ones?"
4. "Where does the eye go first? Is that where the most important thing is?"
5. "Which section feels *disposable* — the one you'd delete to tighten the page?"
6. "What's the most distinctive visual element on this page? Is there one?"

## Example Audit Finding

From [[ux_audit_2026_04_23]], **F-05 [MEDIUM]** — "12+ decorative emoji used as icons — generic-AI-aesthetic tell":

> `site/src/pages/index.astro` — 🗂🧭🚀 (steps), 👩‍💻🏢🎓⚡🔬 (personas), 🎯📐🧩🏛️ (context cards) — 12 emoji used decoratively as section-icons across four sections. This is a strong aesthetic signature of generative-AI-produced landing pages … It works against the vault's "anti-jargon, warm-and-precise" voice.

Also **F-07** (7 sections competing for attention on the homepage) and **F-10** (trust signals buried in the final section) are classic Design Critic calls — composition-level decisions that degrade the page even when each individual element is "fine."

**Why this reviewer owns it**: the Persona ranker captures "does this feel right to me?" but often forgives familiar patterns (emoji-icons are ubiquitous in modern SaaS). Only a reviewer with trained visual judgment will name the pattern as *generic* and recommend custom SVG marks, typographic section anchors, or a ruthless section-count reduction.
