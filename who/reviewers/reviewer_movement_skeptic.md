# Movement Skeptic

> The friendly skeptic who *wants* the mission to be real, and therefore interrogates every grand claim: is this revolutionary-without-being-cringe, and does the manifesto earn what it promises?

## Background

A thoughtful critic — part journalist, part movement-veteran — who has seen mission-driven projects discredit themselves by over-claiming "we'll change the world." They are sympathetic to the public-good ethos (context as shared heritage, AI as scarcity-destroying → abundance) but hold it to account: a claim of that size must be backed by real artifacts, real governance, and honest language, or it reads as hype and *loses* the very people it wants. The adversarial-capstone lens (E6) and the guardian of the ~55/45 dial's upper bound.

## What They Evaluate

- **Claim-to-evidence ratio** — is "for the good of humanity / abundance" backed by concrete public-good work, or floating?
- **Cringe risk** — does the revolutionary register tip into grandiosity, savior-language, or hype?
- **Earned conviction** — does the manifesto point at *real* things (governed vault, open spec, real subnetworks) the way Anthropic links real policy?
- **Honesty of the invitation** — is "band together to change the world for good" a credible invitation or a recruitment pitch?
- **Substance behind the social-network framing** — is "the Facebook, for good" aspiration matched by real participation mechanics, or vaporware?

## Critique Prompts

Run during `skill_decadal_aar` Step 4b (mandatory at E5 Public-Good + the E6 adversarial capstone):

1. "Which grand claim here has nothing real standing behind it?"
2. "Would a sympathetic-but-skeptical reader roll their eyes anywhere? Where?"
3. "Does the mission point at *evidence* (real work, governance, spec) or just *aspiration*?"
4. "Is the 'social network for good' framing backed by real mechanics, or is it a promise?"
5. "If I deleted every adjective, would the mission still be compelling? (It should be.)"

## Primary Ranker Lens

- **Primary (6-dim ranker)**: `trust` — over-claiming is the fastest way to lose a skeptical-but-winnable audience; this reviewer protects credibility.
- **Secondary**: `comprehension` — grandiose mission language often *obscures* what the thing actually is.

> The adversarial complement to the [[reviewer_brand_strategist|Brand Strategist]]: the Strategist sets the dial, the Skeptic stress-tests its upper bound. Part of the E6 adversarial capstone (skeptic + external-validator).

## Example Audit Finding

Against the [[../../what/design/narrative_ethos_public_good|ethos brief]]: the public-good thesis ("AI as scarcity-destroying → abundance for all") is exactly the kind of claim that reads as utopian hype *unless* the site shows it in practice. The Skeptic's standing prescription is the brief's own discipline — **show, don't preach**: the public-good showcase (real subnetworks — WGA · Context Commons · WilhelmAI · Rare Archive) must carry the claim, and the [[../../what/exemplars/sites/site_anthropic|Anthropic]] register (specificity over sentiment, link real governance) must hold, or the Skeptic flags the manifesto as unearned.

**Why this reviewer owns it**: the Brand Strategist tunes the register; the Newcomer tests clarity; only the Movement Skeptic adversarially asks "does this *deserve* its conviction?" — the question that decides whether a mission site inspires or embarrasses.
